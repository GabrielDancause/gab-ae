Understanding Your Mortgage

Principal vs Interest

Early in your loan, most of your payment goes toward interest. As you pay down the principal, more of each payment reduces your loan balance. This is why extra payments early in the loan have such a dramatic impact.

15-Year vs 30-Year

While 30-year loans have lower monthly payments, 15-year loans typically offer lower rates and dramatically less total interest. Consider your cash flow, other financial goals, and investment opportunities when choosing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Your monthly mortgage payment is determined by four main factors: the loan principal (amount borrowed), interest rate, loan term (15 or 30 years typically), and any extra payments. Additionally, your total housing payment may include property taxes, homeowners insurance, and PMI (private mortgage insurance) if you put down less than 20%.
A 15-year mortgage typically offers lower interest rates and you'll pay significantly less total interest, but monthly payments are higher. A 30-year mortgage has lower monthly payments but you'll pay more interest over the life of the loan. Choose based on your monthly budget, financial goals, and how long you plan to stay in the home.
While 20% down payment helps you avoid PMI and reduces your loan amount, it's not always necessary. Some loans allow as little as 3-5% down. Consider your savings, other financial goals, and current interest rates. Sometimes it's better to put less down and invest the difference, especially in a low-rate environment.
Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) is required when you put down less than 20%. It typically costs 0.3% to 1.5% of your loan amount annually. You can usually remove PMI when you reach 20% equity through payments or home value appreciation, though specific rules vary by lender and loan type.
Extra payments can save significant interest and shorten your loan term, but consider your complete financial picture first. Prioritize high-interest debt, emergency funds, and retirement savings. If you have low mortgage rates (under 4%), investing the extra money might yield better returns than the interest saved.
