Home Energy Guide

Solar calculators, electricity cost estimators, BTU sizing, and rate comparisons — the math behind cutting your energy bills and going solar.

Energy is most homeowners' largest recurring expense after the mortgage, and most have no idea if they're overpaying. The average US household spends $2,000+/year on electricity — and that number is climbing. Between rate increases, inefficient HVAC systems, and phantom loads, most homes are hemorrhaging money on energy waste that's entirely fixable.

Solar has crossed the tipping point. In most US markets, a properly sized solar system pays for itself in 6-10 years and generates 20+ years of free electricity after that. But "properly sized" is the key — an oversized system wastes money, an undersized one doesn't eliminate your bill. You need to do the math.

This hub connects you to calculators for solar sizing, electricity cost estimation, HVAC BTU requirements, and electricity rate comparisons by state. Whether you're evaluating solar, sizing a new AC unit, or just trying to understand your electricity bill, the tools are here.

The oversized AC trap: An AC unit that's too large is WORSE than one that's slightly too small. Oversized units short-cycle — they cool the air quickly but don't run long enough to dehumidify. The result: a cold, clammy house that costs more to operate. Use the BTU calculator to size correctly, and resist the temptation to "go up a size."

Frequently Asked Questions

Is solar worth it in 2026?

In most US markets, yes — especially if your electricity rate exceeds $0.12/kWh and you have a south-facing roof with minimal shading. The federal ITC (Investment Tax Credit) still offers 30% off installation costs through 2032. Run the solar calculator with your actual usage data before committing.

Should I lease or buy solar panels?

Buy (or finance) if you can. Solar leases/PPAs save you 10-30% on electricity but the leasing company captures most of the value. Ownership captures the tax credit, increases home value ($15K-$25K on average), and yields better 25-year economics. Only lease if you can't qualify for financing.

What uses the most electricity in my home?

HVAC (40-60%), water heater (14-18%), large appliances (washer, dryer, fridge: 13-15%), and lighting/electronics (10-15%). The biggest bang for your buck: upgrade to a heat pump, add insulation, and switch to LED lighting. These three changes typically reduce bills by 25-40%.

How do I read my electricity bill?

Focus on two numbers: your rate ($/kWh) and your usage (kWh). Multiply them to get your energy charge. The rest is delivery charges, taxes, and fees that you usually can't control. Compare your kWh month-over-month to spot inefficiencies — a spike without a lifestyle change means something's wrong.
