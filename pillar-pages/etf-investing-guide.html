ETF Investing Guide

The single best investment vehicle ever created for individual investors. Navigate expense ratios, category selection, and portfolio construction across 62 ETF research pages.

ETFs democratized investing. Before them, getting diversified exposure to hundreds of stocks cost you actively-managed fund fees of 1-2% annually — fees that compounded into hundreds of thousands of lost dollars over a career. Today, you can own the entire global stock market for 0.03% per year. That's $3 on a $10,000 investment.

But the ETF universe has exploded to over 3,000 funds, and most of them are garbage. Thematic ETFs, leveraged products, and marketing-driven launches are designed to sell, not to perform. 80% of investors need no more than 3-5 ETFs. The hard part isn't buying ETFs — it's knowing which ones to ignore.

This hub covers the ETFs that actually matter: broad market index funds, category-specific picks (bonds, growth, international), expense ratio comparisons, and tools to build an evidence-based portfolio. No hype, no thematic garbage, no "next big thing" ETFs that'll be liquidated in 3 years.

Frequently Asked Questions

ETF vs. index mutual fund — does it matter?

For most investors in taxable accounts, ETFs win due to their tax efficiency (in-kind creation/redemption mechanism avoids capital gains distributions). In retirement accounts, it's a wash — pick whichever has the lower expense ratio. Vanguard's mutual funds and ETFs are the same share class, so they're identical there.

Should I buy VTI or VOO?

VTI (total market, ~4,000 stocks) vs. VOO (S&P 500, ~500 stocks). Historically, their returns are nearly identical because the S&P 500 dominates total market performance. VTI adds small-cap exposure which has a slight theoretical premium. Either is fine — just pick one and stay consistent.

How much international exposure do I need?

Vanguard recommends 40% international, which matches global market cap weights. Most U.S. investors hold 20-30%. The floor should be 20% — home country bias is one of the most common and costly portfolio mistakes. VXUS or IXUS are the simplest ways to get there.

Are thematic ETFs (AI, cannabis, metaverse) worth it?

Almost never. Thematic ETFs are launched when a trend is hot, which means you're buying at peak hype. They charge higher fees (0.50-0.75%), have concentrated holdings, and most get liquidated within 5 years. The few that survive rarely beat a broad market index. They're products designed to sell, not to perform.
