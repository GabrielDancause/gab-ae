Reproductive Health Guide

Evidence-based pregnancy calculators, ovulation tracking, fertility resources, and preventive care tools — because reproductive health decisions deserve data, not guesswork.

Reproductive health is one of the most consequential areas of healthcare, yet it's surrounded by more misinformation than almost any other topic. Fertility myths, outdated medical advice, and algorithm-driven fear get more engagement than peer-reviewed data. That's a problem we can fix with better tools.

Whether you're trying to conceive, tracking your cycle for awareness, or making preventive health decisions, the fundamentals are the same: understand your biology, track the right data, and make decisions based on evidence — not TikTok fertility coaches or your aunt's advice from 1995.

This hub connects you to our best reproductive health calculators and guides — from pregnancy due dates to ovulation prediction, HPV vaccination data, and IVF planning tools. Every resource is built on clinical data and designed for practical use.

The fertility window reality: You can only conceive during approximately 6 days per cycle — the 5 days before ovulation (sperm can survive up to 5 days) plus ovulation day itself. An ovulation calculator identifies this window, but cycle tracking over 3+ months gives you the most reliable prediction for your body specifically.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate are ovulation calculators?

Calendar-based calculators are a reasonable estimate but not precise — they assume regular cycles. For better accuracy, combine with basal body temperature tracking or LH test strips. The calculators work well as a starting point; biological confirmation methods narrow the window further.

Is the HPV vaccine worth getting as an adult?

If you're under 45 and haven't been vaccinated, the evidence supports catch-up vaccination. Efficacy is highest before sexual debut, but the vaccine still provides protection against HPV types you haven't been exposed to. The cancer prevention benefit is one of the strongest in all of medicine.

When should I see a fertility specialist?

If you're under 35 and haven't conceived after 12 months of regular unprotected sex, or over 35 and haven't conceived after 6 months. Earlier if you have known risk factors (irregular periods, endometriosis, prior pelvic surgery, known male factor). Don't wait for an arbitrary timeline if something feels off.

How reliable are at-home pregnancy tests?

Modern home pregnancy tests are 99%+ accurate when used on or after the day of your expected period. Testing too early (before implantation) can give false negatives. If negative but period doesn't start, retest in 2-3 days. False positives are extremely rare and usually indicate early pregnancy loss.
