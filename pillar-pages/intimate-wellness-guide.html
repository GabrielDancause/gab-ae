Intimate Wellness Guide

Lubricant science, product comparisons, and sexual health fundamentals — because this stuff matters and nobody teaches it properly.

Intimate wellness is arguably the most under-discussed area of adult health. Most people's product knowledge comes from whatever's on the drugstore shelf, and most sexual health education stops at "use protection." The result: people use the wrong products, ignore fixable problems, and miss basic health screenings.

Lubricant choice alone has real health implications. Water-based vs. silicone vs. oil-based isn't just preference — it affects barrier contraceptive compatibility, infection risk, and mucosal health. Using the wrong lubricant with condoms can increase breakage rates by 5-10x. This isn't trivial information, and it should be widely available.

This hub collects our evidence-based guides on lubricant types, product comparisons, and broader sexual health topics. Clinical data, practical recommendations, zero embarrassment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does lubricant pH matter?

Vaginal pH is normally 3.8-4.5 (acidic). Using a lubricant with a much higher pH can disrupt the vaginal microbiome, potentially increasing risk of bacterial vaginosis or yeast infections. Look for products with pH 3.5-4.5 for vaginal use. For anal use, a slightly higher pH (5.5-7) is appropriate.

Can oil-based lubricants break condoms?

Yes — oil degrades latex within 60 seconds of contact, increasing breakage risk by up to 10x. This includes coconut oil, vaseline, and oil-based lubricants. Only use oil-based products with polyurethane, polyisoprene (non-latex), or no barrier method. When in doubt, water-based is always safe.

How often should I get STI screening?

CDC recommendations: annual chlamydia/gonorrhea screening for all sexually active women under 25 (and older with risk factors). HIV screening at least once for everyone 13-64, more frequently with risk factors. All sexually active people should discuss screening frequency with their provider based on number of partners and practices.
