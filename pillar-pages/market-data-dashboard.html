Market Data Dashboard

Historical returns, valuation metrics, and real-time market indicators — the data you need to make informed allocation decisions instead of guessing.

Most investors make decisions based on vibes. They buy when markets feel good (expensive) and sell when markets feel scary (cheap). Data doesn't have feelings. This hub gives you the historical context and valuation metrics that separate informed allocation from emotional gambling.

The S&P 500's average annual return since 1926 is ~10.5%. But "average" is misleading — in any given year, the market returns between -37% and +53%. What matters is understanding where we are in the cycle: valuation levels (PE ratios), market concentration (are 7 stocks driving everything?), sector rotation patterns, and volatility regimes.

These aren't tools for timing the market. They're tools for sizing your bets. When the Shiller PE is at 35, you don't sell everything — but you might tilt toward international or value stocks. When volatility spikes, you rebalance into the fear. Data-driven, not prediction-driven.

The CAPE ratio signal: When the Shiller PE exceeds 30, subsequent 10-year annualized returns have averaged 4-6%. When it's below 15, subsequent returns average 10-14%. You can't time the market, but you can set return expectations based on starting valuations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you actually use PE ratios to time the market?

Not for timing — markets can stay expensive for years. But PE ratios are the best predictor of 10-year forward returns. Use them for expectations, not entry/exit signals. If CAPE is 35, don't expect 10% annual returns for the next decade.

Why does market concentration matter?

When the top 10 stocks make up 35%+ of the index, your "diversified" index fund isn't actually diversified. A selloff in mega-cap tech would disproportionately hammer the S&P 500. Consider equal-weight or international diversification when concentration is historically extreme.

What's the VIX telling us right now?

The VIX measures expected 30-day volatility. Below 15 means markets are complacent (often precedes a spike). Above 30 means genuine fear (historically a good time to be buying). It's not predictive, but it's a useful gauge of sentiment extremes.
