Contraception Guide

Evidence-based comparisons of every major contraceptive method — efficacy rates, side effects, costs, and which method actually fits your life.

Contraception is one of the most important healthcare decisions a person makes, yet most people choose their method based on whatever their first doctor suggested years ago. There are 15+ FDA-approved contraceptive methods, each with dramatically different efficacy, side effect profiles, and lifestyle requirements. The "best" method is the one that fits your life — not the one with the best marketing.

The efficacy gap between methods is enormous. Perfect-use failure rates range from 0.05% (implant) to 18% (spermicide alone). Typical-use rates are even more revealing — they show how methods perform in real life, with real humans who forget pills, skip appointments, and make mistakes. LARC methods (IUDs, implants) dominate because they remove human error from the equation.

This hub compares every major method side-by-side, with detailed guides on the most effective options. No judgment, no agenda — just data to support informed decisions.

The typical vs. perfect use gap: The pill is 99.7% effective with perfect use. In reality, 9 in 100 pill users get pregnant annually because of missed doses, timing inconsistency, and drug interactions. IUDs and implants have virtually no gap between perfect and typical use because there's nothing to forget. That's why ACOG recommends LARC as first-line contraception.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which method has the fewest side effects?

The copper IUD is the only highly effective hormone-free option. It can increase period flow and cramping, but has no systemic hormonal side effects. Among hormonal methods, the hormonal IUD (Mirena, Kyleena) delivers the lowest systemic hormone dose because it acts locally. The implant and pill deliver more systemic hormones.

How quickly does fertility return after stopping contraception?

For most methods, fertility returns within 1-3 months. The pill, patch, ring, and IUD removal — ovulation typically returns within 1-2 cycles. The injection (Depo-Provera) is the exception: it can take 6-12 months for fertility to return. There's no evidence that long-term use of any method causes permanent infertility.

Does emergency contraception work for everyone?

Plan B (levonorgestrel) becomes less effective above 165 lbs and may be ineffective above 195 lbs. Ella (ulipristal acetate) works up to ~195 lbs. For higher body weights, the copper IUD is the most effective emergency contraception — 99.9% effective when inserted within 5 days. Weight-based guidance is critical and often overlooked.
