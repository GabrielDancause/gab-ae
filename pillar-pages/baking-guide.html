Baking Guide

Sourdough calculators, hydration science, yeast conversions, and bread-baking fundamentals — because baking is chemistry, not guesswork.

Baking is the most unforgiving form of cooking. You can't taste and adjust mid-process. You can't "eyeball" hydration and expect consistent results. Baking is chemistry with delicious outcomes — and like chemistry, precision matters.

The sourdough renaissance proved something: people are willing to weigh ingredients to the gram, track fermentation times, and obsess over crumb structure. Good. That's how great bread happens. But you need the right tools — baker's percentages, hydration calculators, and yeast conversion charts turn recipes from guesswork into repeatable science.

This hub connects you to our baking calculators and guides — from beginner bread baking to advanced sourdough technique. Whether you're converting a recipe from instant to active dry yeast or dialing in your sourdough hydration, the math is done for you.

The #1 beginner mistake: Using volume measurements (cups) instead of weight (grams). A "cup of flour" can vary from 120g to 160g depending on how you scoop. That 30% variance ruins ratios. Get a $15 kitchen scale and weigh everything. Your bread will improve overnight.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does sourdough starter take to mature?

A new starter takes 7-14 days of daily feeding to become reliably active. It should double in size within 4-6 hours of feeding at peak maturity. Don't give up at day 5 when it smells terrible — that's normal. The good bacteria are winning, they just haven't won yet.

Can I substitute instant yeast for active dry?

Yes — use 25% less instant yeast than active dry (e.g., 7g active dry = 5.25g instant). Instant doesn't need proofing in water first and works faster. Use the yeast converter for exact amounts in any recipe.

Why does my bread collapse after baking?

Usually over-proofing. The gluten structure stretched too far and can't support itself. Other causes: under-baking (the interior wasn't set), too much steam late in the bake, or cutting while too hot (wait 45+ minutes before slicing). Use the poke test — the dough should spring back slowly with a slight indent.

Do I need a Dutch oven for bread?

It's the single most impactful upgrade for home bread baking. The enclosed space traps steam, which creates a crispy crust and better oven spring. A $40 Lodge Dutch oven will dramatically improve your loaves. No fancy bread-specific equipment needed.
