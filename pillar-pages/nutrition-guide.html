Nutrition Guide

Calorie calculators, protein targets, meal estimators, fasting protocols, and food databases — the evidence-based toolkit for eating with intention.

Nutrition advice is a minefield. Every year brings a new villain (carbs, fat, seed oils, lectins) and a new savior (keto, carnivore, plant-based, Mediterranean). Here's what the evidence actually supports: calories determine weight change, protein determines body composition, and food quality determines health outcomes. That's it. The rest is optimization or ideology.

Most people overcomplicate nutrition because complexity sells books and supplements. The fundamentals are boring: eat enough protein (most people don't), eat enough fiber (definitely not), don't massively overconsume calories (the hard part), and eat mostly whole foods. Get these four things right and you're ahead of 90% of the population.

This hub connects you to calculators that remove the guesswork — from calorie targets to protein requirements, meal estimation tools, and fasting tracking. Data-driven eating, not diet dogma.

The protein priority: If you change one thing about your diet, increase protein. Higher protein intake (1.6g+ per kg) preserves muscle during fat loss, increases satiety (you feel fuller), has a higher thermic effect (your body burns 20-30% of protein calories just digesting it), and supports recovery from training. It's the single highest-ROI nutrition lever.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to count calories?

Not forever, but everyone should do it for 2-4 weeks at least once. Most people wildly underestimate their intake (by 30-50%). Counting for a few weeks calibrates your intuition. After that, you can estimate more accurately. Think of it as training wheels — necessary at first, optional once you've learned.

Is intermittent fasting actually beneficial?

For weight loss, IF works primarily by reducing overall calorie intake — there's no metabolic magic. For health, there's emerging evidence that extended fasting windows (16-18h) promote autophagy and improve insulin sensitivity. If IF helps you eat less and you feel good doing it, great. If it makes you binge at your eating window, it's counterproductive.

How much protein can I absorb per meal?

The "30g per meal" limit is a myth. Your body can absorb and utilize much more — studies show muscle protein synthesis benefits from 40-60g per meal in active individuals. However, spreading protein across 3-5 meals optimizes muscle protein synthesis better than cramming it all in one meal. Aim for 30-50g per meal.

What supplements are actually worth taking?

The evidence-supported short list: creatine monohydrate (5g/day — the most studied supplement in sports nutrition), vitamin D (if you're deficient, which most people are), omega-3s (if you don't eat fatty fish 2x/week), and protein powder (for convenience, not necessity). Everything else has marginal or no evidence.
