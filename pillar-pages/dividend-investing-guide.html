Dividend Investing Guide

Build wealth that pays you while you sleep. The complete resource hub for dividend strategies, from Aristocrats to high-yield ETFs — backed by 63 data-driven tools and analyses.

Dividend investing is the closest thing to a "boring" strategy that actually works. While growth investors chase the next 10-bagger, dividend investors compound quietly — and the math is brutal in their favor. Since 1960, reinvested dividends have accounted for 85% of the S&P 500's total return. That's not a typo.

The trick isn't chasing the highest yield (that's a trap — yields above 6% usually signal a company in trouble). It's finding companies with a track record of growing dividends, solid payout ratios, and business models that generate predictable cash flow. The Dividend Aristocrats — companies that have raised dividends for 25+ consecutive years — have outperformed the S&P 500 with lower volatility over virtually every rolling 10-year period.

This hub connects you to our best dividend research: calculators to model reinvestment scenarios, analysis of every major dividend ETF, yield comparisons, and deep dives into individual strategies. Whether you're building a retirement income stream or just want your portfolio to throw off cash, start here.

The Yield on Cost trick: If you buy a stock yielding 3% and dividends grow at 10% annually, your yield on cost doubles to 6% in 7 years and hits 12% in 15 years. This is why entry point matters more than current yield — buy quality dividend growers when they're cheap.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between dividend yield and dividend growth?

Yield is what a stock pays you today (annual dividend ÷ price). Growth is how fast that payment increases each year. A stock yielding 2% with 15% growth will pay you more in 5 years than a stock yielding 5% with 0% growth. For long-term investors, growth almost always wins.

Should I reinvest dividends or take the cash?

If you don't need the income, reinvest. Always. The compounding effect is massive — reinvested dividends accounted for the majority of stock market wealth creation over the last century. Only take cash when you're in the distribution phase (retirement) or when the stock is significantly overvalued.

Is SCHD really the best dividend ETF?

SCHD is the best all-around dividend ETF for most investors — it balances yield (~3.4%), dividend growth (~12% CAGR), and quality screening. But "best" depends on your goal: VYM offers higher current yield, DGRO emphasizes growth, and NOBL tracks Dividend Aristocrats specifically. Use our comparison tools to find your fit.

Are dividends taxed differently than capital gains?

Qualified dividends (from stocks held 60+ days) are taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income). Non-qualified dividends are taxed as ordinary income. This makes qualified dividend stocks particularly powerful in taxable accounts — and makes tax-loss harvesting strategies essential.
