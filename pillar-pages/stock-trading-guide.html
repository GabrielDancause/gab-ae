Stock Trading Guide

Calculators, screeners, and strategy tools for active traders — from options pricing to short interest analysis. Use responsibly.

Let's be honest upfront: most active traders lose money. Studies consistently show that 70-90% of day traders are net unprofitable over any 12-month period. The ones who survive aren't smarter — they have better risk management, lower costs, and the discipline to follow rules they set when they weren't emotional.

That said, trading tools and techniques aren't just for day traders. Options strategies can hedge a long-term portfolio. Short interest data reveals crowded trades. Margin calculators keep you from blowing up. Even if you're a buy-and-hold investor, understanding these tools makes you a more informed market participant.

This hub collects our best trading resources: calculators for options pricing and margin requirements, short interest trackers, stock screeners, and a day trading simulator where you can lose fake money before risking real money. If you must trade actively, at least do it with the right tools.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the minimum I need to day trade?

In the US, the Pattern Day Trader rule requires $25,000 minimum in a margin account to make 4+ day trades in a 5-day period. Below that, you're limited to 3 day trades per rolling 5 days. This rule applies to stocks and options, not crypto or futures.

How does short interest work as a signal?

High short interest (above 20% of float) means a lot of traders are betting against the stock. This creates potential for a short squeeze if the stock rises — shorts are forced to buy, pushing it higher. It's also a contrarian indicator: heavily shorted stocks occasionally recover when the thesis is wrong.

Should I use margin?

For most people, no. Margin amplifies gains AND losses. A 50% decline on 2:1 margin wipes you out completely. If you use margin, keep it below 1.3:1 leverage, use stop losses religiously, and never margin to buy speculative positions. It's a tool for experienced traders, not a way to "invest more."

Are options worth learning?

Yes — but for hedging and income, not speculation. Covered calls on positions you own and protective puts for downside protection are legitimate portfolio tools. Buying naked calls or puts is essentially lottery tickets with a time decay drag. Learn the Greeks before touching options.
