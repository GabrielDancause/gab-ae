Fitness Training Guide

One-rep max calculators, TDEE estimates, workout generators, HIIT timers, and VO2 max tracking — the math behind getting stronger, leaner, and more durable.

The fitness industry thrives on complexity. New programs every month, "muscle confusion," periodization schemes that require a PhD to follow. Here's the reality: the best program is the one you actually do consistently. After that, it's progressive overload, adequate protein, and sufficient recovery. Everything else is optimization at the margins.

That said, the margins matter when you're past the beginner phase. Knowing your one-rep max lets you program intelligently. Knowing your TDEE prevents spinning your wheels in a bulk or cut. VO2 max is the single best predictor of all-cause mortality — cardio isn't optional, it's life extension.

This hub connects you to the calculators and tools that turn guesswork into data-driven training. Track what matters, ignore what doesn't, and let progressive overload do the work.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate are one-rep max calculators?

Within 5-10% for sets of 1-8 reps, less accurate above 10 reps. The Epley formula is most popular; Brzycki tends to be slightly more conservative. Use the estimate for programming — calculate your 1RM from a 3-5 rep set, then program working sets at 70-85% of that number.

Should I train based on TDEE or just track weight?

Start with a TDEE estimate, then adjust based on actual weight changes over 2-3 weeks. TDEE calculators are estimates — your actual metabolism varies. Track weight daily, average weekly, and adjust calories by 200-300 if you're not trending in the right direction. The calculator gets you in the ballpark; reality fine-tunes it.

How often should I test my VO2 max?

Every 8-12 weeks if you're actively training cardio. VO2 max improves quickly in untrained individuals (10-20% in 8 weeks) and more slowly in trained athletes (2-5%). A sub-maximal estimate (Cooper test or 1-mile walk test) is good enough for tracking — you don't need a lab.

Is HIIT better than steady-state cardio?

They serve different purposes. HIIT is time-efficient and improves anaerobic capacity. Steady-state builds aerobic base and is easier to recover from. The research says: do both. 2-3 HIIT sessions per week plus 1-2 longer steady-state sessions is the optimal mix for health and performance.
